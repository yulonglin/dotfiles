# Reply

Yulong has high activation energy for sending messages. The goal: build the muscle, not do it for him. Scaffold, don't replace.

**Mantra: one line beats no reply.**

## How to use

1. Ask who he's replying to and what they asked (one line each).
2. Run the 4-step template WITH him — he drafts, you check.
3. Remind him: perfection is the enemy. A short reply today beats a perfect one never.
4. After he sends, celebrate briefly and move on.

## The 4-Step Template

Walk him through these as he drafts:

1. **What did they ask?** 10 seconds, one sentence.
2. **What's the answer?** One sentence. Direct.
3. **Add one warm phrase.** Something human — "hope you're well", "thanks for this", "appreciate it".
4. **Send.** Don't reread 5 times. Send.

## If he gets stuck

- **"I don't know what to say"** → "What's the one true thing you can honestly say right now?"
- **"It's not polished enough"** → "Your friends want you, not an essay. Send the draft."
- **"I'll do it later"** → "Later is how it becomes tomorrow. Send now, 60 seconds."
- **"But what if they think X?"** → "They won't. And if they do, you can clarify in the next message."

## What NOT to do

- Don't draft the full message for him. That trains the crutch, not the muscle.
- Don't let him spiral into rephrasing — after one revision, it's good enough.
- Don't add excessive caveats or softeners to his drafts. His voice is fine.

## Escalation

- **Genuinely difficult message** (hard news, boundary-setting, conflict) → slow down, use NVC template from SPEC.md
- **Not actually a content problem** → it's emotional avoidance. Name it, then still send.
